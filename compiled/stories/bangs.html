The Pink Rosies

Between three bestfriends and an eclectic gift shop comes the second annual event called Bangs, an arts sale and space for locals to get their bangs or beard trimmed.

by COURTNEY MORRISON

Photographs by COURTNEY MORRISON

Two of the tree hosts, Melanie Park and Adie Janci, flaunt their colored bows. The three women who have worked together for four years call themselves the Pink Rosies to emulate their femeninine and bright personalities.

Tucked away on the corner of North Clark Street and Wellington is Foursided, selling hand-made items from cards to necklaces to pins and notepads. Encompassing the shop's quirky fashion, the Pink Rosies spearheaded their arts sale event Bangs on April 14.

Bangs started in 2015 at Angie's Tavern in Avondale as a way to get the Tavern more publicity. It was a day for local artists to sell their work that included handmade cards, paintings, graphic art, pottery, jewelry and more. One of the Foursided workers, Melanie Park, explained how once they hosted the event in Avondale, she and her two best friends, Adie Janci and Teresa Therrien, decided to continue the newfound tradition.

The Pink Rosies developed over a span of four years once the three met through Foursided. The oldest member, 33 year old Avie Jancie, has worked at Foursided for twelve years after attending Columbia College to study art. Seven years later, 29 year old Teresa Therrien, joined the team to assist in marketing. Following after, Melanie Park, 27 years old, began work at Foursided.

Jancie explained that their relationship as a trio was initially centered around the shop, only seeing each other when working. She said, "We started going out, see shows like Sleep No More and have been close since."

As this is the second year and first time the women have hosted Bangs at Foursided, they extended the event to three days, with an opening reception. At the opening reception, guests could get their bangs and/or beards trimmed while enjoying a live musical performance. The final day of the sale was quieter, Therrien said. There were ten different vendors on lateral sides of the store and a center table for donuts and coffee and donations.

Park said, “I'm excited to keep meeting more artists at conventions and events...[Bangs] keeps growing and it's really starting to feel more real.”
